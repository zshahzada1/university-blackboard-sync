Foundations

Descriptive Ethics

Describes how people actually behave and what moral standards they follow (e.g. professional codes, religious rules). No prescription — just observation.

Normative Ethics

Prescribes how people should behave. Provides action-guiding rules. All eight theories this week are normative. This is what you apply in assessments.

The Eight Normative Theories

Remember the structure for each theory in assessments: (1) define the theory, (2) apply it to the facts, (3) state what it concludes, (4) identify its tensions or limitations in this context.
Ethics of Duties (Deontology)
Immanuel Kant — Foundations of the Metaphysics of Morals
Focus: Duties

Categorical Imperative: Act only on rules you'd want universalised, and always treat humans as ends — never merely as means.

Businesses have a duty to allow stakeholders to influence the corporation; stakeholders are ends, not just resources.

Limitation: Ignores consequences entirely — an act can be "right" even if it causes enormous harm.

Utilitarianism
Jeremy Bentham · John Stuart Mill
Focus: Outcomes

Greatest Happiness Principle: An action is right if it maximises good outcomes for the greatest number of affected people.

Used for cost-benefit analysis. Considers all stakeholders. Consequences are everything.

Limitation: Can justify harming a minority for majority benefit; difficult to measure "happiness" objectively.

Virtue Ethics
Aristotle — Nicomachean Ethics · Alasdair MacIntyre — After Virtue
Focus: Character

Focus on being a good person, not on following rules or maximising outcomes. Possessing virtues (courage, honesty, justice, integrity) is required.

Eudaimonia = human flourishing — the ultimate goal. Profit is one element, but stakeholder wellbeing matters too.

Limitation: How do we explain a good person doing bad things? No clear action-guide for dilemmas.

Ethics of Rights & Justice
John Locke · Jean-Jacques Rousseau · John Rawls — A Theory of Justice
Focus: Rights & Fairness

Locke: Natural rights (life, freedom, property) that all must respect. Rousseau: Social contract — fair hypothetical agreement governing relationships. Rawls: Design society behind a "veil of ignorance" (not knowing your position in it).

Limitation: Rights can conflict; difficult to determine whose rights take priority.

Egoism
Thomas Hobbes — Leviathan · Ayn Rand — The Virtue of Selfishness · Milton Friedman
Focus: Self-Interest

An action is right if it pursues the decision-maker's own interests. Adam Smith's "invisible hand" — self-interest creates wider market benefits through supply and demand.

Friedman: a company's only responsibility is to generate profit for shareholders.

Limitation: Ignores harm to others; the "invisible hand" often fails (e.g. market failures, externalities).

Ethics of Care
Carol Gilligan — In a Different Voice · Virginia Held — The Ethics of Care
Focus: Relationships

Humans are relational and interdependent. Moral obligations arise from relationships and the responsibilities they create. Emotions matter as much as rationality.

Often considered a type of virtue ethics focused specifically on human interaction and mutual dependency.

Limitation: Can lead to partiality — prioritising those close to us over distant strangers.

Discourse Ethics
Jürgen Habermas — Moral Consciousness and Communicative Action
Focus: Dialogue

Ethical conflicts are resolved through open, honest, inclusive dialogue between all affected parties. Those in power should not impose their values on others.

Partly a reimagining of Kantian ethics — still requires an element of universalisability through rational consensus.

Limitation: Requires equal power to participate; in reality, powerful parties dominate discussions.

Postmodern Ethics
Zygmunt Bauman — Postmodern Ethics
Focus: Moral Impulse

Full rejection of universal rules. Ethics is individual and context-specific, located in an internal "moral impulse" — a gut feeling towards others encountered in real life.

Corporate policies and codes are tools that allow decision-makers to avoid genuine personal moral responsibility.

Limitation: If everyone follows their gut, whose impulse prevails? Offers no framework for resolving disagreements.

The Pluralistic Perspective

Crane, Matten & Spence (2019) argue: Don't pick one theory as "the truth." Use multiple theories as a prism — each illuminates a different aspect of the same ethical dilemma. They are complementary, not mutually exclusive.

In assessments, this means: when you apply two theories to a case and they reach different conclusions, that is a feature, not a problem. The tension between them demonstrates critical thinking.

For Task A: apply two theories, show what each concludes, then discuss where they agree, where they diverge, and what the limitations of each are in this specific context.

Case Study: Deutsche Bank & Eric Ben-Artzi

What Happened

2007–10Deutsche Bank held $120–130bn in Leveraged Super Senior (LSS) credit derivatives. They inflated the "gap option" value, concealing up to $12bn in losses — presenting a falsely healthy picture during the financial crisis.
InternalDr Eric Ben-Artzi (Quantitative Risk Analyst, PhD from NYU) discovered the fraud. Reported internally through compliance channels. Was ignored, then dismissed.
2010–11Ben-Artzi reported to the SEC under the Dodd-Frank Whistleblower Program (10–30% award on sanctions over $1m).
2015Deutsche Bank paid $55m fine to the SEC. Crucially: no admission of wrongdoing and no individual executives were prosecuted.
AftermathBen-Artzi awarded $8.25m — publicly rejected it, arguing the fine punished shareholders (victims), not guilty executives. Criticised the "revolving door": Deutsche Bank's lead counsel Robert Rice became Chief Counsel at the SEC.
Personal"Blackballed" from finance. Near-bankruptcy. Relocated to Israel. Eventually accepted the award under financial pressure.

Applying the Three Theories

TheoryBen-Artzi's whistleblowingKey tensions
Utilitarianism Benefits: market integrity, investor protection, deterrence. Harms: Ben-Artzi's career/family, shareholders bore the $55m fine. No executives punished — does this reduce overall utility? Does long-term deterrence restore it? The outcome was arguably suboptimal.
Deontology (Kant) Ben-Artzi had a duty to report — from professional role and universal moral law. Categorical Imperative 1st: "report corporate fraud" can be universalised ✓. 2nd: Deutsche Bank treated investors as means (protecting bonuses) ✗ Morality of the act is independent of consequences — it was right regardless of personal cost. But deontology cannot account for the unjust outcome.
Virtue Ethics Demonstrated courage, honesty, justice, integrity. Showed phronesis (practical wisdom) by escalating step-by-step. Silence would have been cowardice and complicity. Ben-Artzi said he could "feel he was not a thief" — acting virtuously contributed to eudaimonia despite material loss. But virtue ethics cannot tell us which specific action was right.

Workshop Case: GigFlex & OptiRoute

Key Facts

  • GigFlex: UK gig economy delivery platform, ~14,000 drivers classified as independent contractors (no sick/holiday pay)
  • New algorithm "OptiRoute" reduced delivery times 18% but bottom 40% of drivers lost ~£120/week
  • Drivers skipped meals and toilet breaks to protect their rankings; one driver had a road accident
  • GigFlex's response: she was a contractor, responsible for her own road safety
  • 200 drivers wrote an open letter demanding consultation and a minimum earnings guarantee

Applying the Remaining Four Theories

TheoryVerdict on OptiRouteKey Argument
Rights & Justice (Rawls) Morally wrong Behind a veil of ignorance, no one would design OptiRoute. Drivers had no input — violates the social contract. Natural rights to dignity and safety are breached.
Egoism (Friedman) Arguably justified CEO is maximising shareholder value. But: is the invisible hand working? Harm to drivers suggests market failure — consumers benefit while workers bear all the cost.
Ethics of Care (Carol Gilligan) Morally wrong GigFlex depends on drivers; this mutual dependency creates moral obligations beyond contract. Incentivising self-harm (skipped meals, speeding) is a fundamental failure of care.
Discourse Ethics (Jürgen Habermas) Process was wrong Drivers' demand for consultation before algorithmic changes is entirely reasonable. Those affected by decisions must be included in making them — GigFlex imposed its values unilaterally.
Postmodern Ethics (Zygmunt Bauman) Deeply problematic GigFlex used legal contractor language when a driver was injured — hiding behind rules to avoid genuine moral responsibility. Bauman would see this as a corporate policy used to suppress the moral impulse.

Task A Structure Reminder (750 words)

  • Part (a) — Case Summary (~200 words): Misconduct, sanctions, mitigating/aggravating factors. Reference the Disciplinary Orders file.
  • Part (b) — Two Theories (~350 words): Apply each theory, discuss what it concludes, whether sanctions align, and the tensions/limitations.
  • Part (c) — Personal Reflection (~200 words): Was the outcome fair? What does this teach you? How will it influence your future approach?
The 80%+ difference in writing: Don't just describe what theories say — critically evaluate them. Show where they agree and disagree, where they fall short, and what their application reveals about professional ethics. That's the jump from descriptive (60%) to analytical (80%+).
